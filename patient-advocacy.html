The Prior Authorization Problem — What It Means for You

Prior authorization is the process where your insurance company requires your doctor to get approval before they can prescribe a medication, order a test, or recommend a procedure. In theory it controls costs. In practice it delays or blocks care that your doctor has already decided you need.

The 2024 American Medical Association survey of 1,000 practicing physicians found that 93% say prior authorization causes care delays for their patients. 82% say it leads patients to abandon recommended treatments entirely. These are not billing inconveniences. They are clinical outcomes. The treatment your doctor prescribed doesn't happen because an insurance company's system said no — and because fighting that denial takes time that neither you nor your doctor has.[1]

The most devastating finding: 29% of physicians — nearly 1 in 3 — reported that prior authorization caused a serious adverse event for a patient in their care. 23% reported a patient hospitalization. 8% reported a patient's disability, permanent damage, or death.[1]

"Prior authorization requires physicians and their staff to spend an average of 13 hours per week completing prior authorization requests — time taken directly away from patient care."

— American Medical Association, 2024 Annual Prior Authorization Physician Survey. Survey of 1,000 practicing US physicians.

Here is what makes this worse: 82% of prior authorization denials that patients actually appeal are overturned. The insurance company was wrong. But only 0.2% of denied patients ever appeal — because most people don't know they can, don't have the energy, or can't wait for the process to play out while they're already sick.[2]

The AI Hallucination Problem — What It Means for Your Safety

Artificial intelligence is being adopted rapidly across healthcare. Most AI tools used in medical settings today are cloud-based — they send your information to a remote server, generate a response, and return it to your doctor. The problem is that these systems hallucinate.

An AI hallucination in healthcare is not a minor glitch. It is a system confidently generating false medical information — an invented drug allergy, a fabricated prior treatment, a nonexistent test result — that your doctor may not catch if they are reviewing dozens of patients under time pressure.

The legal system has already begun responding. In April 2026, a federal judge in Oregon fined two attorneys $110,000 for submitting court documents filled with AI-generated fake cases and fabricated citations. That same month, the elite law firm Sullivan & Cromwell formally apologized to a US Bankruptcy Court after an AI tool fabricated legal sources in a court filing.

These are attorneys — trained professionals — failing to catch AI hallucinations under the pressure of their workload. If this is happening in law, where citations can be verified in seconds, what happens when a cloud AI invents a detail in your patient chart?

When a cloud AI vendor's system produces a hallucination that leads to patient harm, the vendor invokes their Terms of Service and claims safe harbor. The physician carries the liability. The patient carries the harm.

Why This Is Happening

Your doctor spends an average of 5.9 hours per day in the electronic health record — nearly 60% of their workday. They spend another 2.7 hours on documentation after clinic hours, at home, after the children are asleep. They process 39 prior authorization requests per week on top of their patient load.[4]

This is not a failure of your doctor's commitment. It is a failure of the systems surrounding them. The documentation burden, the prior authorization fights, the administrative overhead — these are system design problems, not physician performance problems. And they have direct consequences for the quality of attention your doctor can give you during your appointment.

A physician who spends 5.9 hours in an EHR and then rubber-stamps an AI-generated summary at the end of a 12-hour day is not being careless. They are being human under an unsustainable workload. The solution is not to blame the physician. The solution is to give them better, safer tools.

What a Safer Architecture Looks Like

The safest AI for healthcare is AI that cannot reach the internet. Not because internet-connected AI is always wrong — but because an AI that can only retrieve information from your actual, verified medical records cannot invent information that isn't there.

When an AI system runs locally on hardware inside your doctor's practice — with no connection to external servers — every answer it generates is grounded in documents that actually exist in your file. It cannot fabricate a drug allergy that isn't documented. It cannot invent a prior authorization approval that was never granted. And if it makes an error, the audit log shows exactly what document it retrieved and why — so the error can be found and corrected.

This is not a theoretical architecture. It is what the Sentinel Node™ does — and it is why the prior authorization documentation a practice produces with local AI is more complete, more accurate, and more defensible than what cloud AI generates from a partial picture of your records.

What This Means for Your Care

A medical practice using the Sentinel Node™ can produce a complete, accurate prior authorization case in seconds rather than hours — drawing from your full, verified medical history, on hardware that never leaves their building.

More complete documentation means fewer initial denials. Fewer denials means less treatment abandonment. Less treatment abandonment means better patient outcomes. And because the AI runs locally — with a full audit trail of every document it retrieved — your physician can verify every output before it goes anywhere.

Your protected health information never leaves your doctor's building. Not to the AI vendor. Not to a cloud server. Not to anyone. The hardware is in the office. The data stays in the office.

That is not a marketing claim. It is a physical fact enforced by the architecture of the system.
