Research Brief — EHR Documentation Burden

Where the 5.9 Hours Figure Comes From — and What the Full Research Shows

The claim that physicians spend 5.9 hours per day in the EHR is one of the most cited statistics in healthcare administration. It is also one of the most misattributed. Here is the exact study it comes from, what it actually measured, what multiple subsequent studies confirmed, and what the complete picture looks like in 2026.

The Primary Source — The Sinsky et al. Study

The 5.9 hours figure originates from a peer-reviewed retrospective cohort study published in the Annals of Family Medicine in 2016, conducted by researchers at the American Medical Association and multiple university health systems. The study is formally titled: "Allocation of Physician Time in Ambulatory Practice: A Time and Motion Study in 4 Specialties."

The researchers analyzed 142 family medicine physicians in a single health system in southern Wisconsin using Epic EHR event logging records collected over three years, validated by direct time-motion observation. This is one of the most methodologically rigorous EHR burden studies ever conducted — it used actual system event logs, not self-reported estimates, and was cross-validated with direct human observation of physician activity.

"Clinicians spent 355 minutes (5.9 hours) of an 11.4-hour workday in the EHR per weekday per 1.0 clinical full-time equivalent: 269 minutes (4.5 hours) during clinic hours and 86 minutes (1.4 hours) after clinic hours. Clerical and administrative tasks including documentation, order entry, billing and coding, and system security accounted for nearly one-half of the total EHR time (157 minutes, 44.2%). Inbox management accounted for another 85 minutes (23.7%)."

— Sinsky C, Colligan L, Li L, et al. Allocation of physician time in ambulatory practice. Annals of Family Medicine. 2016. PMC5593724. doi:10.1370/afm.1678

The "Pajama Time" Problem

The Sinsky study documented 86 minutes (1.4 hours) of after-hours EHR work per physician per day. But subsequent research found the burden has grown. A 2024 study by Rotenstein et al., published in the Annals of Internal Medicine and supported by the AMA EHR Use Research Grant Program, found that primary care physicians now average 2.7 hours of after-hours EHR work daily — nearly double what was measured in 2016.[3]

This after-hours work — colloquially called "pajama time" in the clinical literature — represents documentation time that physicians cannot complete during scheduled patient hours and must carry into evenings and weekends. It directly correlates with physician burnout risk, reduced career satisfaction, and higher intent to reduce clinical hours or leave the profession.

For a primary care physician earning $260,000 per year, 2.7 hours of daily uncompensated EHR work represents roughly $85,000 in lost productive value annually.[5]

What Multiple Studies Have Confirmed

The Sinsky finding has been replicated and extended by multiple independent research teams:

Overhage & McCallie — JAMA Internal Medicine, 2020

A national study of 155,000 US physicians using Cerner EHR log data found that US physicians document approximately twice as much as physicians in other nations, with American outpatient notes approximately four times longer than Canadian equivalents. The researchers estimated that US physicians collectively spent 125 million hours documenting outside office hours in 2019 alone.[4]

Rotenstein et al. — Annals of Internal Medicine, 2024

The most recent large-scale study, conducted across multiple US health systems and supported by the AMA, found that physicians spend 36.2 minutes in the EHR per patient visit — for an appointment scheduled at 30 minutes. This means the documentation burden already exceeds the appointment itself. The study also confirmed 2.7 hours of pajama time daily and found that prior authorizations are a major contributor to EHR inbox burden.[3]

National Electronic Health Records Survey, 2019 (JAMA)

A nationally representative cross-sectional study found that 58.1% of US physicians disagree that the time spent documenting is appropriate and does not reduce time with patients. 84.7% agreed that documentation required solely for billing purposes increases total documentation time. The researchers estimated US physicians spent 125 million hours documenting outside office hours in 2019.[4]

"Documentation burden in healthcare continues to increase. A recent study comparing the period from 2019–2020 to 2022–2023 showed a significant increase in the time primary care physicians spent in the EHR across most tasks."

— PMC National Library of Medicine. Measuring Documentation Burden in Healthcare. PMC11534919. 2024.

What This Means for a Solo or Small Practice

For a family medicine physician seeing 20 patients per day, the math compounds quickly. If documentation averages 36.2 minutes per visit, that is 12 hours of documentation for 20 visits. On a scheduled 8-hour clinic day, that leaves a gap of 4+ hours that can only be filled by working through lunch, staying late, or doing pajama time after the kids are asleep.

This is not a productivity problem. It is a structural system design problem. The EHR was built to satisfy billing requirements and regulatory compliance — not to support clinical efficiency. The documentation content the physician needs to retrieve — a patient's medication history, a prior treatment response, a note from six months ago — is buried in that same system they are spending 5.9 hours navigating.

The Sentinel Node™ does not replace the EHR. It does not eliminate documentation requirements. What it does is eliminate the retrieval burden — the 85 minutes of chart review, the inbox hunting, the tab-switching to find prior treatment history before writing a note or building a prior authorization.

When a physician needs to know a patient's complete medication history before a note, they ask the Sentinel Node in plain language. Across 20 patients per day, that compounds into meaningful reclaimed time. Not 5.9 hours. But measurably less than 5.9 hours.
