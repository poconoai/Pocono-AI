How It Works

Three steps. No IT department required.

1

We install a small server in your office

The Sentinel Node™ ships pre-configured. Your local IT partner or our team connects it to your existing network in a few hours. It looks like a workstation. It acts like a private AI data center.

2

Your documents stay local — and get indexed

Patient charts, clinical notes, case files, and discovery documents are indexed locally on the Sentinel Node. Nothing is uploaded anywhere. Your office network is the only place this data ever lives.

3

Your team asks questions. Gets answers. Saves hours.

A nurse types: "Patient's full prior auth history for Drug X." The answer arrives in seconds, with citations from actual patient records, on any workstation in your office. No cloud call. No delay.

Human-in-the-Loop required: licensed professionals verify all AI-generated outputs before clinical or legal action. The Sentinel Node is a research assistant, not a decision-maker.

Why Not Just Use ChatGPT?

Cloud AI requires your data to leave your building. Ours doesn't.

Every cloud AI tool — no matter what their privacy policy says — requires your patient records or client files to travel over the internet to a remote server to be processed. That transmission is the risk. A Business Associate Agreement promises careful handling after your data has already left your building.

The Sentinel Node makes that journey impossible. It processes everything locally. There is no transmission to promise. There is no server to breach. Your compliance case is architectural — not contractual.

Simulated Workflow — Based on Real EHR Data Patterns

A Tuesday afternoon at a 4-physician family medicine practice.

The following is a simulated workflow constructed from documented EHR burden research (Sinsky et al., 2016; AMA, 2024). No real patient data was used. Results will vary by practice volume and workflow.

8:00 AM

Before: Dr. Chen arrives 45 minutes early to review 22 patient charts manually before the first appointment. She highlights lab values, checks medication lists, and flags abnormal results by hand.

With Sentinel Node: Overnight, the Sentinel Node generated a one-page summary for each patient: recent labs, current medications, open problem list, last visit notes. Dr. Chen reviews the stack in 8 minutes with coffee. She is present for her first patient at 8:05.

1:15 PM

Before: A nurse spends 28 minutes assembling documentation for a prior authorization for a patient's specialty medication — opening four tabs, cross-referencing diagnosis codes, and manually writing a justification letter.

With Sentinel Node: The nurse types: "Prior auth summary for [patient] — specialty medication, complete history." The Sentinel Node returns a cited summary in 40 seconds, pulling from three years of local chart data. The nurse reviews, signs off, and submits. Total time: 4 minutes.

5:30 PM

Before: Dr. Chen stays until 7:00 PM finishing documentation from the afternoon. She misses her daughter's soccer practice. Again.

With Sentinel Node: Documentation drafts were generated during the appointment gaps. Dr. Chen reviews, edits, and signs off by 5:45. She makes the second half of the game.

The Patient Side of the Equation

When physicians reclaim time, patients get a more present doctor.

Physician burnout isn't just a workforce problem — it's a patient safety problem. A doctor spending 5.9 hours on documentation is a doctor spending less time in the room, catching fewer clinical signals, and delivering less of the care they trained for. For rural communities with one physician within 45 minutes, a burned-out practice that closes is a health access crisis.

A physician with 2 hours back per day spends more time per appointment — catching the clinical signal that gets missed when the clock is running. Documentation waits. People don't.

Independent practices that survive burnout don't close. For rural patients — where the nearest alternative may be 45 minutes away — keeping one practice viable is a community health intervention.
