Research Brief & Case Study — Prior Authorization

Prior Authorization Is Costing American Medicine the Equivalent of 100,000 Nurses

That is not a figure of speech. It is the conclusion of a peer-reviewed study in Health Affairs Scholar (September 2024) by researchers from Harvard, McKinsey, and Stanford. The prior authorization crisis is documented, quantified, and getting worse. Here is the complete picture — and what local AI with full patient history access can do about it.

The Scale of the Problem

Prior authorization was designed as a targeted cost-control mechanism. It has become something else entirely. It is now one of the most documented sources of physician burnout, patient harm, and administrative waste in American medicine.

The AMA's 2024 annual survey of 1,000 practicing physicians found that the average practice completes 39 prior authorization requests per physician per week. Physicians and their staff spend 13 hours on this process — nearly two full business days.[1] Forty percent of practices now employ staff whose sole job is prior authorization.[1]

A peer-reviewed study published in Health Affairs Scholar in September 2024, authored by researchers from Harvard, McKinsey, and Stanford Business School, measured the burden across the entire care team. Billing and coding specialists spend 11 hours per week on prior authorizations. Practice managers spend 5 hours. Medical office administrators spend 4 hours. Among clinical staff, nurses carry the heaviest burden at 3 hours per week — and that number is growing.[2]

"Provider respondents reported spending the time equivalent of more than 100,000 full-time registered nurses per year on prior authorization. If the PA process could be automated and half of RNs' time refocused, this would be the equivalent of introducing a little more than 100,000 RNs into the workforce — against an expected shortage of 200,000 to 450,000 RNs in 2025."

— Sahni, Istvan, Stafford & Cutler. Health Affairs Scholar, Vol. 2, Issue 9, September 2024. PMC11425057.

What It Costs Practices in Dollars

An analysis by ReferralMD, cited by Medical Economics, estimated that practices spend roughly $68,000 per physician per year on interactions with health plans, including authorizations, appeals, and peer-to-peer reviews.[3] The Medical Group Management Association found that practice spending on prior authorization staffing jumped 43% between 2019 and 2024, even as reimbursement lagged.[3]

Individual submissions cost $20–$30 each to process manually.[5] Each denied claim then costs $25–$118 to rework.[6] Surescripts data shows each manual prior authorization request consumes approximately 20 minutes of care team time — a figure that automation reduces to 2–3 minutes.[7]

What It Does to Patients

The 2024 AMA survey documented patient harm at a scale that warrants urgent attention. 93% of physicians reported that prior authorization causes care delays. 82% said it leads patients to abandon recommended treatments. More than 1 in 4 physicians — 29% — reported a serious adverse event for a patient in their care attributable to prior authorization, including hospitalization, permanent impairment, or death.[1]

23% of physicians reported a prior-authorization-related patient hospitalization. 18% reported a life-threatening event requiring intervention. 8% reported a patient's disability, permanent bodily damage, or death.[1]

The Denial System Is Broken — And AI Is Making It Worse

Insurers have increasingly deployed AI to accelerate prior authorization denials. A study published in Health Affairs in January 2026 by Stanford researchers found that 82% of appealed prior authorization denials in Medicare Advantage plans are ultimately overturned — meaning the vast majority of initial denials are wrong on the merits.[4]

The deeper problem: barely 0.2% of denied patients ever appeal. Payer AI systems are not making accurate clinical determinations. They are, as the Stanford researchers describe it, betting that almost no one will push back.[4]

The AMA found that 61% of physicians are concerned that payer AI tools increase denial rates — with some AI systems producing denial rates up to 16 times higher than historical norms in certain cases.[1] A 2024 US Senate Subcommittee on Investigations report found one major insurer's post-acute care denial rate more than doubled after deploying its AI system, climbing from 8.7% to 22.7% between 2019 and 2022.[4]

"The process has accumulated complexity well beyond its original scope and now ranks among the most operationally expensive bottlenecks in healthcare revenue cycle management — consuming physician time, straining billing teams, and directly affecting patient access to care."

— Medical Economics, April 2025.

Why Appeals Fail — And What Wins Them

When physicians do appeal a denial, 67% report they don't believe the appeal will succeed. 55% lack the staff resources. 53% say patient care cannot wait for the appeals timeline.[1]

What reverses a denial? Complete, patient-specific clinical evidence. Documented treatment history. Prior responses to the same medication. A well-assembled record of exactly why this patient needs this treatment. The prior authorization fight is fundamentally a documentation fight — and the practice that can produce complete, cited, patient-specific evidence fastest wins more approvals and reverses more denials.

The Regulatory Tailwind

The regulatory environment is shifting toward providers. A January 2024 CMS final rule requires Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, and ACA exchange plans to respond to urgent prior authorization requests within 72 hours and routine requests within 7 days, beginning in 2026. Plans must publish approval and denial rates publicly beginning March 31, 2026 — accountability transparency that did not previously exist.[8] At least 10 states passed prior authorization reform legislation in 2024.

The burden is going to decrease. The practices that build efficient, evidence-based documentation workflows now — and win more approvals before the reformed environment arrives — will be in the strongest position going forward.
