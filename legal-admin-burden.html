The Core Problem — Hours Worked vs. Hours Billed

Bloomberg Law's 2024 Attorney Workload and Hours Survey — conducted January 2025 among 1,054 legal professionals across law firms and corporate legal departments — documented one of the starkest productivity gaps in any profession. Attorneys work an average of 48 hours per week. Only 36 of those hours are billable. The remaining 12 hours disappear into administrative duties, project management, time tracking, and internal coordination.[1]

Clio's 2024 Legal Trends Report, which analyzed over 7 million anonymized and aggregated time entries from legal professionals across tens of thousands of firms, found the gap is even wider when measured against a standard workday. The average lawyer bills just 2.9 hours of an 8-hour day — a utilization rate of approximately 37%.[2] The other 5.1 hours are consumed by work that generates no revenue.

The Revenue Cost of Non-Billable Time

At an average US attorney hourly rate of $313 in 2024, each non-billable hour represents direct revenue the practice will never recover.[2] For an attorney losing 12 hours per week to administrative tasks, that is $3,756 per week — approximately $195,000 per year — in potential billings that never materialize. Even adjusting for the reality that not all reclaimed time would be billable, the magnitude of the loss is substantial for any practice.

Clio's data on collection rates adds another layer. Billing realization averages 86% and collection rates average 88%, meaning 10–15% of potential income is lost to inefficiencies even before accounting for non-billable time.[2] Solo and small firms carry an average of 93 days worth of work that is either unbilled or uncollected at any given time.

"For every eight hours worked, only 2.9 are billable — leaving over five hours each day spent on non-billable tasks. Lawyers are increasingly frustrated, and looking for a solution."

— Clio Legal Trends Report 2024. Based on analysis of 7 million+ anonymized time entries from tens of thousands of legal professionals.

What the Non-Billable Hours Actually Contain

The Bloomberg Law survey found that attorneys spend two out of every eight working hours on administrative duties alone — including tracking hours, managing projects, and coordinating workflow.[1] Clio's breakdown of the full non-billable picture across its 7M+ time entry dataset shows the breakdown across three major categories:

Administrative and clerical work — 19% of the workday

Document management, filing, time entry, billing administration, and internal communications. These are tasks that, as the Bloomberg survey notes, 40% of attorneys find somewhat or very inefficient — yet they consume nearly one-fifth of every working day.[1]

Business development — 33% of the workday

Client intake, relationship management, networking, and proposal preparation. In a profession where clients increasingly expect responses within 24 hours yet only 39% of firms meet that standard, the business development burden is both unavoidable and growing.[2]

Document review and research retrieval

The time attorneys spend searching for prior case materials, client history, prior pleadings, deposition summaries, and precedent research is largely untracked in aggregate billing data — it disappears into the administrative category or simply goes unbilled. This is the retrieval burden — the hours spent hunting across document management systems, email chains, and filing structures to find information the attorney already has somewhere.

The Burnout Reality

The ALM Mental Health by the Numbers 2025 survey found that 77% of attorneys report experiencing burnout.[3] Bloomberg Law's survey found that attorneys felt burned out 42% of the time on average in 2024, with mid-to-senior associates reaching 51%.[1] 73% of attorneys did some work on at least half of their vacation days. Only 3% reported doing no work-related tasks during time off.[1]

The primary drivers cited are consistently administrative: overwhelming workloads, time tracking pressure, and the constant context-switching between legal work and non-legal administrative tasks. The legal profession is not short on work. It is short on time to do legal work because so much of each day is consumed by work that does not require a law degree.

The Document Retrieval Problem — Where Privileged Data Slows the Practice

Every prior authorization a physician needs involves patient history retrieval. The legal equivalent is the document retrieval problem: before drafting a motion, an attorney needs the full case history. Before a deposition, they need every prior statement the witness has made. Before a settlement negotiation, they need every dollar figure and admission in the discovery record. Finding that material — in a system that was built for storage, not retrieval — is where hours disappear.

Cloud-based legal AI tools address this retrieval problem — but at the cost of uploading privileged client communications, work product, and confidential case materials to an external server. Under ABA Model Rule 1.6, attorneys have an obligation to make reasonable efforts to prevent the unauthorized disclosure of client information. Sending privileged documents to a third-party AI system is a disclosure event, regardless of what the vendor's terms of service say.

The Sentinel Node™ Response to Legal Administrative Burden

The Sentinel Node™ addresses the document retrieval component of legal administrative burden — the hours spent searching case files, deposition records, prior pleadings, and client history — without any privileged material leaving the building.

An attorney preparing for a deposition queries the Sentinel Node™ in plain language: "What are every prior inconsistent statement made by this witness across all depositions and written discovery?" The system retrieves the answer in seconds, cited to the source documents, on hardware that has never touched the internet. Work product stays in the building. Rule 1.6 compliance is maintained by architecture, not by policy.

This does not eliminate the administrative burden of billing, time tracking, or business development. But it eliminates the retrieval tax — the hours spent finding material the attorney already has — which is among the most cognitively draining and least-valued administrative tasks in legal practice.
